Different Types of Breaches of Contract

Anyone who enters a legal agreement faces the risk of breach of contract. Although contracts are legally-binding agreements, a party to a contract may not live up to their contractual obligations. In the event of a violation, it is essential to work with an attorney experienced in negotiating and drafting contracts. Let’s look at different types of breaches of contract and how to avoid them.

What Is a Breach of Contract?

The first thing to know is that a contract is a legally enforceable agreement between two or more parties. The elements of a valid contract include:
• Offer by one party
• Acceptance of the offer by the other party
• Exchange of something of value (consideration) between the parties

The consideration usually involves money in exchange for the goods or services offered. In short, a breach of contract occurs when one party fails to fulfill its obligations to the other.

Types of Breaches of Contract

There are many types of breaches of contract, but not all have the same legal effect. The classifications include:

Minor Breach

Also Search For Legal Articles referred to as an “immaterial breach,” a minor breach of contract occurs when the breaching party fails to fulfill some part of its obligation but does not breach a material term of the agreement or prevent the parties from fulfilling their remaining obligations.
A minor breach may not allow the aggrieved party to recover damages because they are typically too small to make a lawsuit practical. For example, a late delivery may not have a legal remedy if the breached party cannot show the delay resulted in financial losses.

Material Breach

A material breach occurs when the contract is undermined in its entirety or one party receives a significantly different result than anticipated. A material breach may involve a failure to (1) perform the obligations stipulated in the contract or (2) perform the contracted obligations on time. When a material breach occurs, the plaintiff is permitted to stop performing its obligations and sue for damages.

Anticipatory Breach

A breach need not occur for the breaching party to be liable. An anticipatory breach of contract is one that has not yet occurred, but one of the parties has indicated through words or actions that it will not fulfill its obligations under the contract. Notably, the non-breaching party must take steps to mitigate its losses because it may not be able to recover any additional damages incurred after being notified of an anticipatory breach.

Bad Faith

Contracts are not only legally binding; they also contain an implied duty of good faith and fair dealing. A party that fails to act honestly, fairly, and in good faith and prevents the other party from receiving the benefits specified in the contract can be held liable for breach of implied duty. Bad faith differs from “breach of contract” in that bad faith is not a violation of any provisions but the intent of the agreement.

Mitigating the Risk of Breach of Contract

When you sign a contract, it may not be possible to prevent a breach altogether because you cannot control the other party's actions. But there are ways to mitigate your risks, such as consulting an experienced business attorney who can help prepare or review the contract. Once the contract is in place, it is essential to monitor contract performance to identify potential problems before they become insurmountable.

Remedies for Breach of Contract

While contract law varies from state to state, the plaintiff in a breach of contract claim may be able to recover damages such as:
• Compensatory damages to restore the plaintiff to the financial position they would have been if the breach had not occurred.
• Consequential damages will compensate the injured party for any reasonably foreseeable future losses (e.g. lost business).
• Liquidated damages are specified in the contract when damages of a potential breach are difficult to foresee, making an estimate necessary should a breach occur.
• Equitable remedies may also be available for breach of contract, such as:
Specific performance is a court order directing the breaching party to fulfill and perform its contractual obligations when monetary damages are insufficient to cover the plaintiff’s losses, and the value of the breaching party’s obligation is difficult to determine. Rescission invalidates the contract and returns the parties to their position before entering the contract because no other adequate remedy is available, and the plaintiff cannot obtain monetary damages.

The Takeaway

Contracts formalize the agreement between two or more parties concerning goods or services in exchange for money. When a breach of contract occurs, it takes an experienced business attorney to protect your interests and, hopefully, find an amicable solution.
